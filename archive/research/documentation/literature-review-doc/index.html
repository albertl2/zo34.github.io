Literature Review AKA Critical Review Essay

Aug 9, 2015

Personal Disclaimer

The following is a concatenation of materials that I have gathered and put together for my own edifcation. I have supplied links at the bottom of this page. If you use this material please ensure that you also link back to the originators of the materials.

Personal Purpose

I have compiled this document as a guide for assembling stand-along literature reviews that are meant to aid my personal understanding of different fields of research. With that goal in mind I have tried to keep this document focused on its primary goal of elucidating the function and form of literature reviews in scientific endeavors.

Document Key

Some parts of this apply to only a stand-alone review paper or publication, and others apply to theses, research proposals, and others. The following key of symbols will aid in showing which pieces of this document are set apart for specific purposes. Sentences or sections will be marked at the beginning with one of these bolded symbols.

rp - research proposal

ts - thesis

When should you conduct a literature review?

  1. Write a subject review paper to determine the “state of the art” 12.
  2. Write the introduction and discussion of a research paper 13.
  3. Enter into a new area of research 1.
  4. rp Write a research proposal 132.
  5. Apply existing research.
  6. ts As part of a thesis introduction or chapter 32.
  7. To become familiar with a specific field of research.

What is the context of a literature review (when it does not stand by itself)?

A literature review may fit in to an introduction in the following manner.

  1. Motivation * Overall motivation to study a particular set of problems * May include a few citations of a survey, a book, or some authoritative general reference * includes a research question or thesis statement
  2. Literature Review - Should contain all citations except those from the Motivation section.
  3. ts Contributions of the research effort, paper, or thesis (chapter) that specifically address problems identified in the Literature Review section.
  4. rp Implications of further research that will, once completed, specifically address problems identified in the Literature Review section.

What is the function/benefit of a literature review?

  • Summarize and give a general overview of a body of research that you are unfamiliar with 12
  • Evaluate the literature, showing what has been done 12
  • Show relationships between different sources (e.g., differences in methodology) 1
  • rp Show how published work relates to your work 1
  • ts Provide justification and background for the research to be undertaken 3
  • To give light to new ideas for use in your research 2
  • To help determine where there are problems or flaws in existing research 2

You are undertaking a literature review for two reasons: first to identify and summarize existing research on a topic you have a question about; and second, to evaluate that research as it applies to your research question. … [L]iterature reviews are usually conducted as a preliminary step to designing and conducting your own research study. You don’t want to reinvent the wheel; you want to build your research on what already exists. That is the nature of [research and development]: finding out what has been done and building something new on that foundation.

There are several side benefits of conducting literature reviews: exposure to current research in your field, understanding of various research processes, ability to evaluate those processes, familiarity with the style and forms of academic and professional writing 4.

Literature reviews must be:

  • Accurate: citations must be correct and findings attributed to authors correct[ly] 1.
  • Complete: the review must include all important papers (but not every paper written on the topic) 1 as well as every source cited 3.

What is “the Literature”?

  • Journal articles: most[ly] up to date, but still at least two years old 1. These provide up-to-date technical information about a given topic 3.
  • Internet sources: avoid citing these directly and use them only to lead to more reputable sources 3.
  • Conference proceedings: typically contain the latest research not yet published in paper form 1. These are generally less reliable than journal papers. As such,always cite journal versions if available 3.
  • Government and corporate reports: good sources for commissioned research 1. These may also identify lines of research that organizations may be interested in funding 3.
  • Theses and dissertations: limited use, may be difficult to obtain, researcher typically lacks experience 1. Also, portions of research presented may have to be treated with caution due to the researcher’s lack of experience 3.
  • Books: less up to date, but excellent starting points 1. Do not include citations of textbooks not in the literature review 3.
  • Specialized magazines: These may be considered as reputable as a journal and often provide a good starting point for research 3.

What kinds of questions do literature reviews answer?

  • What is known about the area of inquiry? 13
    • key concepts
    • factors
    • variables
  • What are the relationships between the key concepts, factors, and theories? 13
  • What are the current [relevant or] complementary theories? 13
  • What are the inconsistiencies and other shortcomings [of the existing knowledge]? 13
  • What needs further testing because evidence is lacking, contradictory, or limited? 13
  • What designs or methods are faulty? 13
  • Why study this question further? 13
  • What contribution will the present work make? 13

How do you focus your research efforts most effectively?

Know precisely what you are looking for. Before you begin finding sources it is important to know exactly what kinds of information you are looking for. If you are unfamiliar with a topic, take some time to read a text-book or “for dummies” compilation. This will familiarize you with the topics and language in your subject of interest.

Continue refining your chosen reading until you know exactly what you are looking for. Be careful to record what you’ve read and important terms, concepts, etc. so that you won’t be lost when you try and recall a source or idea.

To drill into this, see Research Questions for Literature Reviews4 in the Sources section below.

How do you parse the most important sources?

Avoid these traps.

  • Try not to read everything. Read only the most relevant work instead.
  • Do not just read; write as well. Writing is a way of thinking and drafts are a way of refining thought.
  • Do not fail to record bibliographic information. Remember that you’ll be assembling references at some point.
  • Do not organize the review chronologically. Instead, organize it by ideas.
  • Do not fail to answer questions–AND–do not answer questions in a general manner.

Do review papers have abstracts? Yes, but there are two kinds.

  • Informative–these are used for research reports. They summarize the study, including the findings and conclusions.
  • Descriptive–these are used for reviews. They summarize the subject of the review and the approach the reviewer has taken in the coverage of the subject. This type of abstract does not report original findings.

A good literature review is focused. Remember the purpose of the review. Then, read and write with purpose.
